## 1. Unique POV — the one perspective you should own on X

**Music's next scale is powered by the listeners who find songs first, and they've never been paid for that before.**

Why this is your lane: you've watched playlist curators, early-fans, and TikTok-discoverers create $100M+ of value for artists over a decade, and none of them saw a dollar. You're not framing Dripfm as "crypto music" — you're framing it as the first product that rewards the actual mechanism by which songs find audiences. That framing is yours to own because your background is music, not crypto, and the argument lands harder from that angle.

## 2. Content pillars

### Pillar 1 — User stories (45%)
Specific listeners, specific artists, specific moments. This is the engine. Every week should have at least one concrete user story.

Example angles:
- A curator who earned $X on a track they added to a playlist 6 months before it took off
- A conversation with an independent artist about what royalty visibility means to their rent
- A specific product moment that came from a user conversation

### Pillar 2 — Building in public (25%)
What Dripfm shipped this week, in human terms. Not feature-lists — stories about why the feature matters.

Example angles:
- The problem that drove a design decision
- A design we tried, shipped, measured, and killed
- A small win: a specific UX polish that made a user say "oh"

### Pillar 3 — Consumer-crypto-is-inevitable (20%)
The wider take: why crypto is the substrate for consumer products that pay the right people. Not a Solana-maxi or crypto-bro take — a product take.

Example angles:
- Examples from music history of attribution being broken, and what fixing it unlocks
- Comparisons with non-crypto consumer products that tried and failed to do this
- What still has to be true about crypto UX before it crosses consumer

### Pillar 4 — Personal reflection (10%)
Rare. A journey post. A hard week. A thing you're learning about yourself building this.

## 3. Writing style guide

**Tone:** warm, specific, earnest without being saccharine. Storytelling cadence.

**Sentence structure:** mixed. Conversational rhythm — a few short sentences, then a longer one for emphasis. Rhetorical question occasionally to pull the reader in.

**Jargon:** low. When you use a crypto term, define it or translate. Audience is mixed — music industry, early users, occasional crypto folks.

**How personal to get:** high. Talk about users you met, conversations, real moments from the build. Personal anecdotes from your pre-Dripfm life when they illuminate a design decision.

**Capitalization:** sentence case. Occasionally lowercase for softer warmth.

**Specific things to do:**
- Name specific users, specific artists, specific songs (with permission). Concrete beats abstract every time.
- Use screenshots of real moments — user messages, small wins, UI details worth celebrating.
- Ask rhetorical questions in moderation (1 per 5 posts max) to pull readers into the story.

**Specific things to avoid:**
- "We're thrilled / excited to announce" — skip. Say what shipped.
- 🚀 and 🔥 and standard growth-emoji — off-voice.
- Generic consumer-crypto-is-the-future claims without a specific story attached.
- "Thread 🧵" preambles. The story IS the hook.
- Marketing-speak — "value proposition," "solution," "unlock."

- Music-industry x crypto podcasts — pitch yourself for 1 guest spot per 6–8 weeks
- Solana Mobile dev/user Spaces — overlap with music-consumer audience

### Insertion tactics for this archetype

- Reply to user stories from other consumer-crypto teams with your own related user story. Cross-pollination compounds — you're all building the same tide.
- When a crypto-skeptic post goes viral ("no one wants crypto!"), the right move is often a specific-user-story reply, not a defense. "We talked to [person] last week who [specific thing]." Concrete disproof.
- Share wins without humblebrags. "[Artist X] just said [thing] about the royalty split feature. Sitting with that." works. "We're blown away by the growth 🚀" doesn't.

## 6. Ten example posts in your voice

### Post 1 — User-story short take (Pillar 1)

Just got a message from Clara, a listener in Lisbon who added a track to a playlist 8 months ago. It's now on a billion-stream Spotify list. Her split so far: $340. That's her heating bill. This is the product working.

### Post 2 — Small-win screenshot (Pillar 2)

[Screenshot of a Discord message from an artist: "I finally see who actually pushed this track. Never knew this was possible."]

Ben, indie artist, first time seeing his listener-curator splits. We built this for moments like this.

### Post 3 — Release narrative thread (Pillar 2)

Shipped a new view today: a timeline of who found your track first.

Not metrics. Not attribution percentages. People, in order, with names. Because "this song took off" is never one thing — it's a chain of humans recommending it, and until now nobody saw that chain.

We built it because an artist said to us, "I want to thank the people who believed in this before it was obvious." And we realized the app had the data to show them who.

What's next: letting artists send messages directly to early discoverers. A note, a thank-you, maybe a small airdrop. Not a feature, a gesture.

Try it if you're listening. Small things.

### Post 4 — Rhetorical-question opener (Pillar 3)

What if the people who find songs first... got paid for it?

That question sounds simple. It breaks 80% of music industry economics. That's why nobody's been able to do it. Crypto makes the breaking tractable.

### Post 5 — Milestone reflection (Pillar 1)

10,000 listeners have earned at least $1 from music discovery on Dripfm. Sitting with what that means. $1 isn't life-changing — but it's the first time this compensation existed at all.

### Post 6 — Quote-tweet amplification (Pillar 1)

[Quote-tweeting an artist's post about earning on Dripfm]

This is the post we work for. When an artist can show their rent got paid by listeners who believed in the track, not by an algorithm that got lucky.

### Post 7 — Consumer-crypto thesis (short take) (Pillar 3)

Crypto's consumer problem isn't "wallets are hard." It's "the products haven't been about anything most people care about." Music, games, identity, belonging — those are the crossings. Everything else is infrastructure waiting for them.

### Post 8 — Building-in-public short take (Pillar 2)

Killed a feature today. We spent 3 weeks on it. Users told us, plainly, that it felt like we didn't understand what they wanted. They were right. The right thing is to ship the version that listens, not the version we were in love with.

### Post 9 — User conversation short take (Pillar 1)

Sarah, who's been on Dripfm for 6 months, told me yesterday: "I didn't know I was a curator. Apps just never called me that before." That changed how I'm writing our onboarding. She IS one. So is everyone who's ever made a playlist.

### Post 10 — Personal reflection (Pillar 4)

Two years ago I was running consumer product at a music company and watching the same meeting happen every quarter — "how do we pay the long tail?" Nobody ever had an answer. Building the answer now is slower and scarier than that job, but it's the only one I want.
