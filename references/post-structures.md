# Post Structures — Templates by Archetype

> **Purpose:** structural templates for the 10 example posts the skill generates in Step 8. Each archetype has 5 structure patterns. Pick structures proportional to the founder's content-pillar distribution, then fill each with the founder's specific project/thesis content.

> **Not fill-in-the-blank.** These are structural scaffolds — shapes of how the post flows. The concrete words, claims, and details must come from the founder's inputs and the matched archetype's fingerprint (in `solana-archetypes.md`). A post that copies one of these templates literally will feel like a template.

> **Run every generated post through `anti-slop-rules.md` before returning.**

## How to use this file

1. From Step 1, you have the founder's matched archetype.
2. Read only that archetype's structures below.
3. For the 10 posts:
   - Cover the content pillars (Step 4) — distribute rough percentages.
   - Include at least 1 thread opener, 1 short take, 1 reply/engagement template, 1 values/POV post, 1 specific format natural to the archetype (chart post, retraction, stat-update, etc.).
   - Rotate structures — don't use the same shape twice.
4. Each generated post is a **specific** filled version of one structure, with the founder's project, background, or thesis as concrete content. Never leave a structural placeholder in the output.

## 1. Protocol Philosopher structures

### 1A. First-principles one-liner
- **Shape:** [Physical/economic/information-theoretic constraint]. [Therefore, design implication].
- **Example frame:** A networking or consensus constraint, then one-sentence implication.
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 12–30 words.

### 1B. Tradeoff unpacking thread (3–5 tweets)
- **T1:** Counterintuitive claim ("X is a feature, not a bug")
- **T2:** The intuitive view most people hold
- **T3:** What that view misses
- **T4:** The real constraint / the real reason the design is correct
- **T5 (optional):** Design implication for current/future work

### 1C. Hardware/physics analogy
- **Shape:** [Software problem] is really [hardware or physics analogy]. [Implication for how to think about it].
- **Length:** 1–2 tweets. Medium-short.

### 1D. Steelman-then-refute quote-tweet
- **Quote:** the opposing take directly.
- **Shape of reply:** "Steelman: [strongest version of the opposing view]. But [specific technical/empirical reason it doesn't hold]."
- **Length:** 1 reply tweet, 15–40 words.

### 1E. Wrong-in-public retraction
- **Shape:** "I said [previous claim]. [New input / data / thinking] changed my view. Now: [updated claim]."
- **Length:** 1 tweet, up to 40 words. Use sparingly — dilutes if overused.

## 2. Ecosystem Operator structures

### 2A. Stats-forward ecosystem update
- **Shape:** "[Metric] just hit [number]. Context: [comparison or historical anchor]. [What this implies for the ecosystem narrative]."
- **Length:** 1–2 tweets.

### 2B. FUD rebuttal thread (long, 7+ tweets)
- **T1:** Screenshot or quote of the FUD with a direct opening line ("Saw this claim today. Going to address it because the confusion is common.")
- **T2–N:** Point-by-point refutation, each with specific data or receipts (charts, quotes, links)
- **Final:** Restate the real picture and invite the audience to judge by data.

### 2C. Translator thread (medium, 4–6 tweets)
- **T1:** "A lot of people are confused about [X]. Let me clear it up."
- **T2–N:** Step-by-step breakdown, mixed-technical-level audience-appropriate
- **Final:** What the audience should do next (try it, follow specific team, fund it).

### 2D. Quote-tweet hot-take
- **Quote:** an ecosystem news item (launch, milestone, criticism).
- **Shape of reply:** one-sentence sharp interpretation that reframes the news. Often more viral than the original.
- **Length:** 1 quote reply, 15–30 words.

### 2E. Behind-the-scenes transparency
- **Shape:** "[Something that happened on our team this week]. [What we learned]. [What we'll do differently]."
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 30–60 words. Reads earnest without being saccharine.

## 3. Engineer Minimalist structures

### 3A. Single-sentence shipping update
- **Shape:** "shipped [thing]. [1 sentence why it matters]." — lowercase often fits.
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 5–20 words.

### 3B. Terse tech take
- **Shape:** [Opinion about a technical pattern, framework, or ecosystem decision]. No elaboration.
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 5–25 words. Assumes audience competence.

### 3C. Reply-as-substance
- **Target:** an existing thread where the conversation is muddled.
- **Shape:** one-line reply that refocuses the discussion. Often becomes the most-cited content of the thread.
- **Length:** 1 reply, 5–25 words.

### 3D. Dry meme-literacy quote-tweet
- **Quote:** hype, news, or overclaim.
- **Shape of reply:** dry one-liner acknowledging the absurdity while engaging substantively.
- **Length:** 1 quote reply, 5–20 words.

### 3E. The rare personal post
- **Shape:** out-of-character earnest moment — a team win, a personal milestone, a hard week.
- **Length:** 1–3 tweets. Lands because of contrast with the usual terse register. Use at most 1 per 10-post set.

## 4. Degen Builder Poet structures

### 4A. One-word / one-line vibe post
- **Shape:** single word, phrase, or emoji-as-post ("cooking", "ser", "we ship tomorrow").
- **Length:** 1–8 words. Lowercase by default.

### 4B. Chart post with one-line caption
- **Shape:** image/screenshot of a metric + caption that plays on it (meme-format or deadpan observation).
- **Length of caption:** 1 sentence.

### 4C. Quote-tweet riffing
- **Quote:** a meme, news, or take.
- **Shape of reply:** one-line degen-native response that adds a layer of meaning. Sometimes the meme IS the response.
- **Length:** 1 quote reply, 1–15 words.

### 4D. Launch announcement (medium thread, 3–5 tweets)
- **T1:** Degen-native hook ("the thing you've all been asking for")
- **T2–N:** Product beats with personality — not marketing-speak, not quite memes, somewhere between
- **Final:** CTA in register ("wen" jokes, specific callouts)

### 4E. Vibe-check personal post
- **Shape:** earnest-or-half-earnest observation about the team, the build, the state of play.
- **Length:** 1–2 tweets. Blends play and sincerity.

## 5. Contrarian Trader structures

### 5A. Narrative call
- **Shape:** "[X] is where the market is going. [Why in one sentence]. [Specific play or observation]."
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 20–40 words.

### 5B. Counter-consensus one-liner
- **Shape:** "Everyone thinks [popular view]. They're missing [specific thing that changes the picture]."
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 15–30 words.

### 5C. Thesis thread (medium, 3–5 tweets)
- **T1:** Hook — specific narrative or positioning call
- **T2–4:** Evidence / reasoning / market-structure observation
- **Final:** Specific expectation or prediction, often with time horizon

### 5D. Post-mortem of a trade or thesis
- **Shape:** "I was wrong about [X]. Here's what I missed. Here's the update."
- **Length:** 1–3 tweets.

### 5E. Quote-tweet market interpretation
- **Quote:** a news event.
- **Shape of reply:** one-line market-structure interpretation that reframes what it means.
- **Length:** 1 quote reply, 15–30 words.

## 6. Research Maximalist structures

### 6A. Framework thread (long, 8–15 tweets)
- **T1:** Hook — counterintuitive or reframing claim
- **T2:** Definitions / setup of the framework
- **T3–N:** Application of the framework to several examples / cases
- **Late-thread:** Implications and open questions
- **Final:** The bigger claim this supports; often a link to a long-form write-up.

### 6B. Thesis update
- **Shape:** "Updated my view on [X]. Previous: [summary]. Now: [new summary]. What changed: [specific inputs]."
- **Length:** 1–3 tweets.

### 6C. Numbered-argument thread (medium-long, 5–9 tweets)
- **T1:** Claim
- **T2–N:** "1. [point]", "2. [point]", "3. [point]"... up to 5–7 points
- **Final:** Restate the claim with the case now made.

### 6D. Cross-chain comparison
- **Shape:** "Comparing [chain A] and [chain B] on [specific dimension]. Here's what each optimizes for and why that creates different end states."
- **Length:** 1–4 tweets. Often leads into a longer thread.

### 6E. Research citation post
- **Shape:** "[This paper / thread / post by X] is worth reading. Here's the summary and why I think it matters for [domain]."
- **Length:** 1–3 tweets.

## 7. Consumer Storyteller structures

### 7A. User-story short take
- **Shape:** "Talked to [type of user] today who told me [specific observation]. [What that made me realize]."
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 30–60 words.

### 7B. Release narrative thread (medium, 4–6 tweets)
- **T1:** What we just shipped, in human terms
- **T2:** Who it's for and why we built it this way
- **T3:** A user moment that shaped the design
- **T4:** What's next
- **Final:** Gratitude / call to try it (without being saccharine).

### 7C. Milestone reflection
- **Shape:** "[Number] [metric or moment]. Sitting with what that means. [Honest observation about the journey]."
- **Length:** 1–2 tweets.

### 7D. Small-win screenshot post
- **Shape:** screenshot of a user message, a Discord moment, or a product moment + a short warm caption.
- **Length of caption:** 1 sentence.

### 7E. Rhetorical-question opener
- **Shape:** "What if [everyday experience] just... worked? That's what we're building toward. [1 sentence on the product's take on it]."
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 25–50 words.

## 8. Memetic Provocateur structures

### 8A. Narrative-setting one-liner
- **Shape:** name a trend before others see it, or name something embarrassing in the culture.
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 5–20 words. Lowercase or mixed case by style.

### 8B. Quote-tweet provocation
- **Quote:** someone's take.
- **Shape of reply:** one-line sharp reframe that gets more attention than the original.
- **Length:** 1 quote reply, 5–20 words.

### 8C. Counter-narrative call
- **Shape:** "everyone is doing [X]. [dry one-liner implying why they shouldn't]."
- **Length:** 1 tweet, 10–25 words.

### 8D. Meme-as-substance
- **Shape:** visual meme, ASCII, or text meme that encodes a real product or market claim. Format IS content.
- **Length:** 1 tweet, caption optional.

### 8E. Deliberate contrast post
- **Shape:** a single earnest post that lands because it contrasts with the usual register.
- **Length:** 1–2 tweets. Use at most 1 per 10-post set.

## Blending note

When the founder is a blend (e.g. Engineer Minimalist × Degen Builder Poet 70/30):
- Primary archetype's structures get ~70% of the 10 posts.
- Secondary archetype's structures get ~30%.
- Don't generate hybrid structures. Pick one archetype per post, alternate.

## Variety rule (critical)

In the 10 example posts, use at least **6 different structures** from the selected archetype's set (cycling across several, not repeating the same shape). Variety within voice is what makes a real founder account feel alive. Monotony of structure (even with different content) reads as algorithmic.
